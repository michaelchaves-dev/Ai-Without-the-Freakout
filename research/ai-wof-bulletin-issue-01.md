## Abstract

Large language model workflows commonly pursue improved performance through additive scaling: more context, more tokens, more retrieved information, and greater computational allocation. Although this approach can improve model capability, it can also introduce redundancy, increase inference cost, and consume limited computational resources unnecessarily.

Quantum Subtraction Theory proposes a complementary engineering approach. Rather than assuming that additional information is always beneficial, it investigates whether redundant or nonessential information can be removed before inference while preserving semantic integrity, operational intent, and critical context.

This initial benchmark evaluates the Core Compression Protocol, designated QCP-1, across four representative enterprise language-processing workloads. The benchmark produced an average token reduction of 70.4%, with observed reductions ranging from 67.4% to 77.6%. Approximately 745 aggregate baseline tokens were reduced to 218 compressed tokens, eliminating approximately 527 tokens while preserving 100% of the critical concepts designated by the evaluation rubric.

These initial findings suggest that subtraction-first context optimization warrants continued investigation as a practical method for reducing inference overhead while maintaining usable semantic fidelity.

## Why This Research Matters

Every unnecessary token consumes some combination of computation, memory bandwidth, energy, processing time, and financial resources.

As artificial intelligence adoption expands, efficiency becomes more than a cost-control objective. It becomes a question of scalability, accessibility, and responsible resource allocation.

The long-term objective of Quantum Subtraction Theory is to help artificial intelligence systems accomplish more with less—not by restricting useful capability, but by identifying and removing waste before that waste reaches the inference layer.

This research treats computation as a resource to be conserved, not merely consumed.

## 1. Introduction

Modern large language model systems have largely advanced through addition.

- More parameters.
- More context.
- More retrieved documents.
- More tokens.
- More compute.

This additive model has produced substantial improvements in artificial intelligence capability. However, it has also encouraged workflows in which information is frequently accumulated without first determining whether every component is necessary for the task being performed.

Larger context windows do not automatically guarantee better contextual quality. A prompt may contain relevant information while also containing repetition, excessive framing, procedural noise, low-value explanation, and material that does not meaningfully influence the requested output.

When these elements are processed together, the model must allocate resources to both the essential signal and the surrounding redundancy.

Quantum Subtraction Theory, or QST, explores an alternative engineering philosophy: remove unnecessary information before inference while preserving the information required for accurate operation.

The theory does not propose that less information is universally better. Nor does it reject model scaling, larger context windows, or advanced computing infrastructure.

Instead, QST asks a narrower and measurable engineering question:

> How much information can be safely removed before meaningful capability, context, or semantic integrity begins to decline?

Within this framework, subtraction is not treated as simple shortening. It is treated as structured signal isolation.

## 2. Research Objective

The purpose of this benchmark was to evaluate whether subtraction-first preprocessing could significantly reduce prompt size while preserving operational context across representative enterprise artificial intelligence workloads.

The benchmark was designed to examine whether QCP-1 could:

- Reduce total token consumption
- Preserve designated critical concepts
- Maintain semantic and operational intent
- Preserve readability
- Produce repeatable optimization results
- Reduce estimated inference overhead
- Perform consistently across different workload categories

The benchmark was not designed to establish universal performance across every language model, prompt type, or production environment.

Its purpose was to determine whether the underlying subtraction-first hypothesis demonstrated enough measurable promise to justify expanded testing.

## 3. Engineering Philosophy

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has been driven by remarkable progress in model architecture, computational infrastructure, and semiconductor technology. As adoption continues to accelerate, efficiency becomes an increasingly important engineering objective alongside capability.

Quantum Subtraction Theory investigates whether meaningful improvements can be achieved not only through expanding computational resources, but also through using those resources more deliberately.

Its central premise is that carefully removing redundant or nonessential information before inference may preserve semantic integrity while reducing computational overhead.

This research is motivated by the belief that efficiency and capability are complementary goals rather than competing priorities. Reducing unnecessary computation has the potential to lower operational costs, improve scalability, increase accessibility, and make more effective use of available computing resources.

The objective is not to oppose large-scale computing infrastructure. The objective is to reduce unnecessary dependence on brute-force resource allocation when disciplined preprocessing, reasoning, and context design may accomplish the same task more efficiently.

Accordingly, the long-term goal of Quantum Subtraction Theory is to develop evidence-based methods that help artificial intelligence systems accomplish more with less through measurable optimization and responsible engineering by subtraction.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Benchmark Environment

The initial QCP-1 benchmark evaluated four representative enterprise language-processing workloads.

Each source prompt was processed using a structured subtraction-first optimization procedure. The procedure was designed to identify and remove redundant language, duplicated instructions, unnecessary framing, nonessential qualifiers, and other material that did not contribute directly to the operational objective.

The optimized version was then compared with the original prompt.

### 4.2 Workload Categories

The benchmark included the following workload classes:

| Workload | Representative Function | Optimization Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Customer Support | Issue resolution and response generation | Repetition, excess framing, procedural language |
| Legal | Document analysis and obligation identification | Dense phrasing, duplicated context, noncritical language |
| Property Management | Operational review and anomaly identification | Repeated conditions, administrative wording, contextual noise |
| Technical Documentation | Instructional and system-oriented processing | Redundant explanation, repeated specifications, verbose formatting |

These categories were selected to represent different levels of structure, density, repetition, and operational risk.

### 4.3 Compression Method

QCP-1 applied a subtraction-first preprocessing sequence that prioritized the preservation of:

- The requested task
- Material constraints
- Named entities
- Operational conditions
- Required output structure
- Critical facts
- Domain-specific terminology
- Dependencies between instructions

Material was removed only when it was determined to be nonessential to the prompt’s operational meaning under the benchmark rubric.

### 4.4 Evaluation Criteria

Each benchmark prompt was evaluated using the following criteria:

- **Token Reduction**: The percentage difference between the baseline prompt and the compressed prompt.
- **Critical Concept Survival**: Whether every concept designated as operationally necessary remained present after compression.
- **Semantic Integrity**: Whether the compressed prompt retained the meaning and objective of the original prompt.
- **Context Preservation**: Whether the relationships, conditions, and dependencies needed to complete the task remained intact.
- **Readability**: Whether the compressed prompt remained interpretable and usable.
- **Repeatability**: Whether the subtraction procedure could be consistently applied across distinct workload categories.
- **Estimated Cost Reduction**: The potential reduction in token-dependent inference expenditure associated with the observed compression.

This benchmark measured prompt-side token reduction. It did not claim that billing savings would always correspond perfectly to the compression percentage, as actual cost depends on model pricing, output length, caching behavior, architecture, and provider-specific accounting.

## 5. Benchmark Results

The QCP-1 benchmark produced the following aggregate results:

| Metric | Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Average Token Reduction | 70.4% | Primary benchmark outcome |
| Token Reduction Range | 67.4%–77.6% | Observed variation across workloads |
| Critical Concept Survival | 100% | No designated critical concepts lost |
| Aggregate Baseline Tokens | ~745 | Combined original prompt volume |
| Aggregate Compressed Tokens | ~218 | Combined optimized prompt volume |
| Tokens Eliminated | ~527 | Absolute reduction achieved |
| Workloads Evaluated | 4 | Representative enterprise prompt classes |

The average result indicates that fewer than three out of every ten baseline prompt tokens remained after QCP-1 processing.

The strongest observed reduction was **77.6%**.

The lowest observed reduction was **67.4%**.

Even at the lower boundary, the protocol removed more than two-thirds of the original prompt volume while retaining all concepts designated as critical under the benchmark rubric.

## 6. Key Findings

Several findings emerged from the initial evaluation:

- Token reduction exceeded the initial baseline expectation across all four workload categories.
- QCP-1 achieved an average token reduction of 70.4%.
- Compression performance remained within a relatively narrow range despite differences among the evaluated workloads.
- No designated critical concepts were lost under the benchmark evaluation rubric.
- Prompts containing greater repetition and procedural framing demonstrated greater optimization potential.
- Dense enterprise documentation contained substantial removable language without requiring removal of the underlying operational meaning.
- The benchmark supports further investigation of subtraction as an independent preprocessing and context-engineering discipline.

The results do not demonstrate that every prompt can be reduced by approximately 70%.

They demonstrate that the evaluated prompts contained a substantial amount of removable informational overhead and that the benchmark protocol successfully identified much of it without losing designated critical content.

## 7. Engineering Discussion

### 7.1 Redundancy as Inference Overhead

Prompt redundancy is often treated as harmless because the information remains technically relevant or because additional context appears safer than omission.

However, information can be relevant without being necessary.

A prompt may repeat the same instruction in multiple forms, provide more background than the task requires, restate already established constraints, or surround a simple operational request with extensive narrative framing.

Each of those elements adds tokens that must be transmitted, parsed, attended to, and processed.

At sufficient scale, small inefficiencies accumulate into substantial operational overhead.

### 7.2 Subtraction as Signal Isolation

QCP-1 treats prompt optimization as a signal-isolation problem.

The purpose is not merely to shorten the text. The purpose is to identify the minimum sufficient representation of the task.

In this context, subtraction attempts to increase informational density:

> Informational density increases when operational meaning is preserved while unnecessary language is removed.

A shorter prompt that loses a critical condition is not an optimization.

A shorter prompt that preserves the complete operational objective with fewer tokens may represent a genuine efficiency improvement.

### 7.3 Reason Before Resources

The dominant response to increasing artificial intelligence demand has often been to expand capacity: larger models, wider context windows, additional accelerators, and more extensive infrastructure.

These investments remain important and have enabled much of the field’s progress.

However, infrastructure expansion should not eliminate the responsibility to examine how efficiently that infrastructure is being used.

QST proposes that engineering judgment should precede unnecessary resource consumption.

Before allocating more tokens, more context, or more compute, a system should first determine whether the information already present can be refined.

### 7.4 Efficiency and Accessibility

The significance of token efficiency extends beyond individual prompt cost.

Reduced inference requirements may contribute to:

- Lower operating expenses
- Faster processing
- Greater request capacity
- Improved performance on limited hardware
- Reduced dependence on premium models
- Broader access for smaller organizations and independent developers
- More efficient use of constrained compute and semiconductor resources

These potential benefits require additional empirical validation. However, they provide a meaningful reason to continue studying subtraction-first optimization.

## 8. Limitations

This benchmark represents an initial internal evaluation and should be interpreted within that scope.

The principal limitations include:

- A limited initial sample size
- Four representative workload categories
- A single benchmark version
- No broad multi-model validation
- No independent external replication
- No formal blinded human-evaluator study
- No direct measurement of energy consumption or hardware utilization
- No production-scale latency study
- No longitudinal assessment of repeated compression
- No evaluation of every possible prompt domain

Critical concept survival was measured according to the benchmark’s designated rubric. It should not be interpreted as proof that no subtle semantic differences existed under every conceivable evaluation method.

Similarly, estimated token-related savings should not be interpreted as a guaranteed one-to-one reduction in total artificial intelligence operating cost.

These limitations do not invalidate the initial results. They define the boundary of what the current evidence supports.

## 9. Future Research

The next phases of Quantum Subtraction Theory research will expand the benchmark’s sample size, evaluation depth, and architectural scope.

Planned research areas include:

- **QST Version 2**: Expanded benchmark volume, refined scoring, stronger statistical reporting, and additional workload variation.
- **Adaptive Compression**: Compression intensity that changes according to prompt structure, risk, domain, and semantic density.
- **Negative Step Zero**: Preventive subtraction performed before conventional preprocessing, routing, or model selection begins.
- **TITAN Architecture**: A broader efficiency architecture combining subtraction, caching, routing, budget controls, memory design, and model escalation.
- **Relay Raise**: Controlled transfer of a task between leaner and more capable models only when complexity or risk requires escalation.
- **Context Architecture**: Methods for preserving relationships and task dependencies while reducing total context volume.
- **Quantum-Inspired Routing**: Decision systems designed to allocate each task to the least resource-intensive model capable of completing it reliably.
- **Memory Compression**: The reduction and refinement of persistent context without eliminating information necessary for continuity.
- **Multi-Model Validation**: Testing QCP performance across multiple commercial and open-weight language models.
- **Independent Replication**: Providing sufficient methodology and benchmark documentation for outside researchers and engineers to reproduce, challenge, or extend the findings.

## 10. Conclusion

The initial Core Compression Protocol benchmark provides preliminary evidence that subtraction-first preprocessing can substantially reduce prompt size while preserving designated critical concepts across representative enterprise language-processing workloads.

QCP-1 achieved an average token reduction of 70.4%, with observed results ranging from 67.4% to 77.6%. Approximately 527 of 745 aggregate baseline tokens were eliminated, while 100% of the concepts designated as critical under the evaluation rubric survived compression.

These findings do not establish subtraction-first preprocessing as a universal solution. They do establish a credible basis for continued experimentation.

As artificial intelligence systems consume increasing quantities of compute, energy, memory, and advanced semiconductor capacity, optimization cannot remain limited to building larger systems.

It must also include improving how deliberately existing resources are used.

Quantum Subtraction Theory advances a simple but consequential engineering proposition:

> Progress in artificial intelligence may come not only from determining what more can be added, but from rigorously measuring what no longer needs to remain.
